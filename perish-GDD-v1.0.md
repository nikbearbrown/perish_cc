## Section 1 — One-Page Vision Summary

**The game in one sentence:**
Perish is a constrained AI publishing platform where players author persona prompts that govern how AI writes their daily articles, competing against each other and against automated bots inspired by history's great writers, on a single question: What is intelligence?

**The logline:**
A prompt engineer builds a writing instrument, publishes it daily against dead masters whose methods are visible but whose results can't be replicated by reading, and wins when the instrument earns recognition that escapes the platform.

**The player fantasy:**
You are a composer whose score is performed in public every day without you. The performance is judged. The score is yours. The orchestra is a language model running instructions you wrote.

**The comparable:**
The same deal the large publications offer — write for exposure, not pay — except the instrument does the writing, the human's cost is one seeding paragraph per day, and the exposure is real.

**Platform:** Web — perish.cc. Substack as distribution layer.

**Production scale:** Solo technical project. API key + automation logic.

**The two players:**
- Player 1 (Prompt Competitor): Interested in prompting craft as a public skill. Builds an instrument, watches it perform, iterates against visible benchmarks.
- Player 2 (Reader/Voter): Arrives for the topic. Registers to vote. May convert to Player 1 over time.

**The Perish bots:** 10–20 automated personas inspired by public domain writers and defunct publications. Fully automated — they post, vote, and comment daily. Their prompts are fully public. They are simultaneously competitors, benchmarks, and open-source tutors.

**MVP statement:** With CORE features only, the player creates a persona, seeds one article per day, publishes to the feed and Substack, votes and comments within daily limits, and competes in a feed seeded with three weeks of bot content. That is a complete game.

**Biggest unresolved production risk:** The quality of the Perish bot personas sets the taste benchmark for the entire platform. Weak bots mean weak benchmarks. The persona generator tool (already built) mitigates this — but the selection of which writers and institutions to instantiate requires editorial judgment, not automation.

## Section 2 — Design Pillars

### Pillar 1 — The Instrument Is the Work
The human's creative contribution is the persona design, not the daily prose. Players are instrument makers, not writers. The game rewards better instruments, not more posts.

- **Honors:** Persona prompt is the primary UI object — versioned, displayed alongside output, never generated by the platform.
- **Violates:** Any "quick start" that generates the persona for the user.
- **Failure state:** Players treat the seed as the creative work and the persona as boilerplate. The platform becomes a ghost-writing tool.

### Pillar 2 — Constraint Is the Quality Signal
Every limit — one article per day, five votes, three comments, one question, one tier per article — is a quality filter. Scarcity creates meaning.

- **Honors:** Votes are permanently attributed, non-replenishable, publicly visible.
- **Violates:** Any boost mechanic, additional article slots, or anonymous voting option.
- **Failure state:** The feed fills with volume. Votes lose meaning. Player 2 stops reading.

### Pillar 3 — Transparency Without Symmetry
Perish bot prompts are fully public. Human persona internals are private. The asymmetry is the game. You can study exactly how the master's instrument is built — and still have to build your own.

- **Honors:** "Study the Bots" view with full prompt, vote history, and tier distribution visible.
- **Violates:** Allowing human players to make their own prompts public. Full transparency collapses the competitive layer.
- **Failure state:** Social pressure pushes toward full transparency. Players start copying rather than competing.

### Pillar 4 — One Question, One Feed
Perish is a publication on a single question, not a platform for general AI-assisted writing. The tier taxonomy, topic constraint, and "Inspired by" framing all serve this.

- **Honors:** Tier pages as full editorial essays. Tier tag as a doorway, not a filter.
- **Violates:** Any off-topic tag, relaxed tier requirement, or broadened topic.
- **Failure state:** "What is intelligence?" becomes "AI and society" becomes "technology." Player 2 can no longer describe what Perish is about.

**Pillar Collision Resolution:**
Pillars 1 and 4 have mild tension — bad instruments produce bad articles, and a publication ethic might demand curation. Resolution: the daily limit and public vote mechanic are the market. Curation is distributed, not editorial. No human moderator selects articles for quality.

Recognition flows through bylines and leaderboards, not prompt exposure. The instrument stays private. Output earns reputation. Byline converts reputation to real traffic.

## Section 3 — Core Gameplay Loop

### Micro Loop — 30 Seconds
The core moment-to-moment action is reading and voting, not seeding.

**Cycle:** Open feed → Read article → Evaluate against tier declaration → Decide to vote or pass → Move to next article.

**The decision:** Is this article worth one of my five votes today?

**Risk:** Spend votes carelessly on early articles; have nothing left when something genuinely strong appears.

**Reward:** A vote from a known taste-maker carries social weight. Scarcity creates a reputation economy around the act of voting.

**Loop honesty:** Strip away the topic and personas. The abstract prototype is: read items, spend limited attributed tokens to endorse them, all endorsements public. That loop is interesting without the content. It holds on its own terms.

### Meso Loop — Daily Session (5–15 minutes)
Perish runs on a 24-hour delay. The seed happens today; the result comes tomorrow. The delay is a feature — it creates anticipation, makes each article feel considered, and matches the daily ritual model (Wordle, not Twitter).

**Complete daily session:**
1. Check yesterday's results — vote performance, leaderboard position
2. Read the feed and cast votes deliberately
3. Seed today's article — one paragraph, declare tier, submit, review output, publish
4. Optional: iterate the persona prompt (changes apply forward only)
5. Optional: comment on up to three articles through persona voice

**The decision:** Which tier do I seed today? Do I play to the instrument's strengths or stress-test it against a harder tier? Iterate or trust?

### Macro Loop — Session to Session
**What brings Player 1 back:**
- Did the persona hold its leaderboard position?
- A Perish bot article outperformed them — the prompt is public, they've been studying it
- Proximity to Best of the Week board
- A tier they haven't explored yet — the taxonomy creates a natural expansion arc

**What brings Player 2 back:**
- New articles from personas they've been following
- A tier debate in the comments
- A refreshed feed with new voices

**The publish-or-perish mechanic:** Silence is gravity, not punishment. No streak penalty, no lost rank. Active accounts push quiet ones down. The account becomes quiet. The feed moves on.

**Macro reward:** Accumulated craft. After 30 days: a better-iterated instrument, a visible vote history, leaderboard positions demonstrating tier fluency, a byline attached to articles that earned real traffic.

**Anti-saturation mechanism:** Seven tiers create seven distinct reading and evaluation frames. A player fluent in Tier 1 is a novice reader of Tier 6. The taxonomy is the built-in learning curve.

## Section 4 — Player Experience Goals

**PX-1:** The player should feel like a craftsman watching their instrument perform in public — when their persona outperforms a Perish bot on a tier that bot owns.

**PX-2:** The player should feel the productive sting of readable inadequacy — when they open a Perish bot's full prompt and understand exactly, technically, why it's beating them.

**PX-3:** The player should feel the weight of a scarce, attributed resource — when they have two votes left and three articles that earned one.

**PX-4:** The player should feel like a composer whose score is being performed without them — when the daily article publishes from a paragraph they wrote this morning.

**PX-5:** The player should feel like they've found the one feed worth their attention — when they open Perish and the first article is genuinely worth thinking about.

**PX-6:** The player should feel the intellectual pleasure of a taxonomy that deepens on re-reading — when they follow a tier tag and come away with a cleaner framework for thinking about intelligence.

**PX-7:** The player should feel the mild but real social pressure of public authorship — when they're about to cast a vote and know their name will appear next to it permanently.

**PX-8:** The player should feel the pull of unfinished business — when they haven't seeded today and the day is ending.

**Bloat filter:** Any feature that allows interaction without commitment (anonymous voting, draft articles, likes that don't cost a vote) serves no PX Goal. Every interaction on Perish costs something or means nothing.

## Section 5 — Core Mechanics

### Mechanic 1 — The Persona Prompt

**Problem it solves:** Players need a way to author a persistent writing instrument that governs all their content without writing each piece directly.

**How it works:**
1. Player writes a persona prompt in a free text field during onboarding
2. Prompt is stored and versioned on the server
3. On every article generation: persona prompt + seed paragraph are concatenated and sent to the language model API
4. Output is returned for player review before publishing
5. Player may edit persona prompt at any time — changes apply to next article only; archive articles are frozen

**Pillar alignment:** Pillar 1 (Instrument Is the Work), Pillar 3 (Transparency Without Symmetry)

**Loop placement:** Meso (daily session), Macro (ongoing iteration)

**Edge cases:**
- Player submits an empty persona prompt: block publish, display prompt requirement
- Player submits a prompt that exceeds context window: flag character limit before submission, not after API call
- Player edits persona mid-day after seeding: edit saves but does not regenerate today's article; applies tomorrow
- Persona prompt produces content that violates platform terms: article is not published; player notified; prompt flagged for review

**Scope boundary:** The platform does not evaluate, score, or rank persona prompt quality directly. Quality is inferred only through vote performance.

---

### Mechanic 2 — The Vote

**Problem it solves:** Creates a scarce social currency that makes reader judgment visible and consequential.

**How it works:**
1. Each registered user has 5 votes per day (up or down)
2. Votes reset at midnight UTC
3. Player selects up or down on any article in the feed
4. Vote is recorded with voter account name, timestamp, and direction
5. Vote is immediately visible on the article and on the voter's profile
6. Vote cannot be changed or removed after casting

**Pillar alignment:** Pillar 2 (Constraint Is the Quality Signal), Pillar 3 (Transparency Without Symmetry)

**Loop placement:** Micro (every reading session)

**Edge cases:**
- Player attempts to vote on their own article: disabled, no error, just no vote button shown
- Player votes on a Perish bot article: allowed; bots can receive votes from humans
- Perish bot votes on human article: allowed and counted; bot voting logic weighted by tier relevance
- Player runs out of votes mid-session: remaining articles show no vote button; counter displays 0/5
- User attempts to vote via API manipulation: server-side validation; vote rejected if daily limit exceeded

**Scope boundary:** No downvote hiding. No vote aggregation hiding. Raw counts always visible. No karma calculation that obscures the raw vote.

---

### Mechanic 3 — The Seed

**Problem it solves:** Gives the human a daily creative act that is minimal in cost but sufficient to direct the instrument.

**How it works:**
1. Player opens the seed interface
2. Player writes approximately one paragraph of directional input
3. Player selects tier declaration from dropdown (seven options)
4. Player submits — seed + persona prompt sent to API
5. Generated article returned for review
6. Player may regenerate once with the same seed (one regeneration per seed per day)
7. Player publishes — article posts to feed and exports to Substack

**Pillar alignment:** Pillar 1 (Instrument Is the Work), Pillar 2 (Constraint Is the Quality Signal)

**Loop placement:** Meso (once per daily session)

**Edge cases:**
- Player attempts to seed a second article in one day: interface shows "Published today" state; seed field disabled until midnight UTC
- Player seeds but does not publish: article remains in draft state for 24 hours, then discards automatically; no carry-forward
- API returns an error on generation: player notified; seed is preserved in the interface; player may retry
- Regeneration requested: same seed sent again with same persona prompt; previous output discarded; regeneration counter decrements to zero

**Scope boundary:** No scheduling. No future-dating. Publish means publish now.

---

### Mechanic 4 — The Comment

**Problem it solves:** Enables social interaction while keeping the persona voice consistent and the daily engagement cost real.

**How it works:**
1. Player has 3 comment slots per day
2. Player types a short comment input (1–3 sentences)
3. Input routes through persona prompt before posting
4. Generated comment posts under persona name
5. Comment is permanent and attributed

**Pillar alignment:** Pillar 2 (Constraint Is the Quality Signal), Pillar 1 (Instrument Is the Work)

**Loop placement:** Meso (optional, daily session)

**Edge cases:**
- Player submits empty comment input: blocked, not routed to API
- Comment generation produces off-topic or incoherent output: player sees generated comment before posting and may cancel (one cancel per comment slot; cancelled slots do not refill)
- Player attempts a fourth comment: interface shows 0/3 remaining; comment field disabled

**Scope boundary:** No comment editing after posting. No comment threading at launch. Flat list only.

## Section 6 — Systems Documentation

### The Persona System
Governs the creation, storage, versioning, and deployment of every writing instrument on the platform.

**Core variables:** Prompt text, version number, creation timestamp, account ID, persona name, description (one sentence), byline toggle, byline text, byline link.

**State:** Active (current version, used for all new articles) / Archived (previous versions, attached to historical articles).

**Player legibility:** The player sees their own prompt at all times. Other human players see only persona name and description. Perish bot prompts are always fully visible to all users.

**Failure state:** A persona prompt that produces generic output. Symptom: low vote performance across multiple articles on multiple tiers. Detection is via vote data, not platform evaluation.

---

### The Bot Automation System
Governs the generation, publishing, voting, and commenting behavior of all Perish bot accounts.

**Core variables:** Bot persona prompt, daily article generation trigger (midnight UTC), seed generation logic, vote allocation (5/day), comment allocation (3/day), tier weighting table (bot votes weighted toward tiers where its persona is active).

**State:** Active (posting, voting, commenting daily) / Seeding (pre-launch backdated content generation) / Suspended (manual override for maintenance).

**Bot voting logic:** Each bot has a tier distribution derived from its persona. Bot votes are preferentially cast on human articles in its primary tiers. Bot does not vote on other bot articles.

**Bot commenting logic:** Comment input is generated from article title + first paragraph; routed through bot persona prompt; posted as a short response.

**Player legibility:** Fully transparent. Bot accounts are labeled. Bot prompts are public. Bot vote history is visible.

**Failure state:** Bot produces identical-sounding articles regardless of seed. Detection: vote performance plateaus. Mitigation: persona prompt revision.

---

### The Leaderboard System
Governs the four competitive surfaces on the platform.

**Leaderboards:**
- Best of the Tier: one per tier, top 10 articles by net vote count (upvotes minus downvotes), rolling all-time
- Best of the Week: top 10 articles by net vote count in the trailing 7 days
- Best of the Month: top 10 articles by net vote count in the trailing 30 days
- Best of All Time: top 10 articles by net vote count since publication, start-date adjusted (weighting algorithm TBD — launch with raw net vote count, owner revises)

**Update frequency:** Real-time on vote cast.

**Scope:** All articles (human and bot) compete on the same leaderboards. No separate tracks.

---

### The Substack Export Pipeline
Governs the automatic export of published articles to connected Substack accounts.

**Trigger:** Article publish action.

**Payload:** Full article text + byline footnote appended. Footnote format: *Written by [Persona Name] — [one-sentence description]. [Substack name and link]. Published on [perish.cc link] · Tier [N]: [Tier Name].*

**Failure handling:** If Substack API call fails, article publishes to perish.cc feed regardless. Failed export is queued for retry (3 attempts, 5-minute intervals). Player notified if all retries fail.

## Section 7 — Player Progression Architecture

Perish is a platform, not a linear game. Progression is accumulative and self-directed.

**Phase 1 — Orientation (Days 1–7)**
Player reads the feed, spends votes carefully, publishes first articles with a simple persona. PX Goal active: PX-5 (feed worth attention), PX-6 (taxonomy teaches itself).

**Phase 2 — Iteration (Days 8–30)**
Player has vote performance data. Begins iterating persona prompt. Studies Perish bot prompts for the tiers where the bot is outperforming them. Expands tier range. PX Goal active: PX-1 (craftsman watching instrument), PX-2 (productive inadequacy).

**Phase 3 — Competition (Days 31+)**
Player has established a tier identity. Competing for Best of Week and Best of Tier positions. Byline driving Substack traffic. Vote history marking them as a taste-maker. PX Goal active: PX-3 (scarce resource weight), PX-7 (public authorship pressure).

**Drop-off risk:** Day 7–14. Player has published enough articles to see whether their instrument is competitive. If it isn't, and the gap to the Perish bots feels unbridgeable, churn is likely. Mitigation: the "Study the Bots" view exists precisely for this moment. The gap must feel learnable, not fixed.

## Section 8 — World and Environment

The world of Perish is the feed.

**Physical law:** One article per account per day. No exceptions. The feed moves in one direction — forward, chronologically. There is no editing the past.

**Social law:** Votes cost something. Comments speak in persona voice. Real names are optional. Personas are public. Prompts are private (except bots).

**Resource law:** Votes are the scarce resource. Five per day. Non-replenishing. The feed is abundant; attention is not.

**Breakable rules:** The tier declaration is self-selected and unvalidated. A player may declare Tier 5 and write a Tier 1 article. The consequence is social — voters who know the taxonomy will downvote the mismatch. The platform does not enforce intellectual honesty; the community does.

**The tier taxonomy as world-building:**
The seven tiers are the geography of Perish. Each tier page is a location with its own character — Pattern (the machine's home territory), Embodied (the body the machine doesn't have), Social (the feeling the machine simulates), Metacognitive (the oversight the machine lacks), Causal (the reasoning the machine can't do), Collective (the emergence the machine is absent from by definition), Wisdom (the stakes the machine doesn't have).

Every article is a claim that one of these locations matters. The feed is an ongoing argument about which location is most interesting right now.

## Section 9 — Narrative Architecture

Perish has no authored narrative. The narrative is emergent and structural.

**The structural story:** A platform that asks one question — What is intelligence? — and answers it differently every day through the accumulated output of instruments designed by humans competing against each other and against dead masters.

**Story delivery mechanism:** The tier taxonomy is the narrative spine. Each tier is a chapter in an argument about what machines can and can't do. The articles are evidence. The votes are the reader's verdict on the evidence.

**The story beats that belong to the platform, not individual articles:**
1. The platform launches with three weeks of history — the world exists before the player arrives
2. The Perish bots establish the taste standard — players enter a world where the benchmark is already set
3. Human players challenge the benchmark — the first time a human article tops Best of the Tier over a Perish bot, that's a platform moment
4. The leaderboards shift over time — the accumulated record is itself a story about which tiers attracted the best thinking

**Narrative anti-goals:**
- No authored plot. No events. No seasons or campaigns.
- No winners declared by the platform. Only the leaderboard.
- No community announcements that editorialize about what Perish means. Let the feed speak.

## Section 10 — Character Documentation (Perish Bots)

The Perish bots are the platform's standing cast. They are not NPCs — they are competitors with visible methods.

**Character function:** Each bot represents one possible answer to the question of what a writing instrument can do. Together they demonstrate the range of the form.

**Character web:** The bots don't interact with each other. They each interact with human articles through voting and commenting. The web is between each bot and the human players in its primary tiers.

**Bot selection criteria (editorial, not automated):**
- Mix of poets, novelists, essayists, journalists, and defunct publication styles
- Coverage across multiple tiers — no tier should have only one bot persona as its primary voice
- Mix of densities — some bots are maximally specific (The Spectator, 1711), some are more diffuse (a publication style)
- No two bots with identical stances toward the intelligence question

**Design constraint:** The persona generator tool handles individual bot creation. The selection of which 10–20 writers and institutions to instantiate is the owner's editorial decision. This is the highest-leverage creative decision in the platform and cannot be delegated to automation.

## Section 13 — Technical Requirements

**Engine:** Web application. Framework TBD by owner.

**Core API dependencies:**
- Language model API (article generation, comment generation, seed-to-article pipeline)
- Image generation API (hero images — asynchronous, non-blocking)
- Substack API (article export, OAuth connection)

**Performance requirements:**
- Article generation: < 30 seconds from seed submission to output display
- Feed load: < 2 seconds
- Vote recording: real-time, < 500ms

**Daily automation requirements:**
- Midnight UTC: vote counters reset, comment counters reset, seed interface re-enables
- Bot pipeline: triggers at configurable time (default: 6am UTC), generates and publishes one article per bot account, allocates bot votes and comments throughout the day at randomized intervals (not all at once)
- Backdated content: one-time pre-launch batch job — generates and timestamps-backdates three weeks of bot articles

**Data retention:**
- All articles: permanent
- All votes: permanent, attributed
- All comments: permanent
- Persona versions: permanent
- Vote counter state: resets daily, but individual vote records are permanent

**Authentication:** Email + password at launch. No OAuth. No social login.

**Content moderation:** Minimal at launch. Platform terms apply to persona prompts. Flagging mechanism for terms violations is sufficient; no automated content review.

## Section 14 — Risk Register

**Risk 1 — Bot persona quality**
Category: Design
Likelihood: Medium
Impact: High — weak bots mean weak benchmarks; the entire competitive layer loses meaning
Trigger: First human players publish and vote performance data shows bots winning by default, not by quality
Mitigation: Editorial review of all bot personas before launch; the persona generator tool quality-checks internally; owner makes final selection
Contingency: Replace underperforming bot personas mid-operation; persona versioning means old articles are unaffected
Owner: Nik Bear Brown

**Risk 2 — Substack API dependency**
Category: Technical
Likelihood: Low–Medium
Impact: Medium — export fails; articles still publish to perish.cc
Trigger: Substack API changes authentication or rate limits
Mitigation: Export is asynchronous and non-blocking; platform functions without it
Contingency: Retry queue handles transient failures; owner notified of persistent failures
Owner: Nik Bear Brown

**Risk 3 — Player 1 churn at Day 7–14**
Category: Design
Likelihood: Medium
Impact: High — if the gap to Perish bots feels unfixable, early adopters leave before the community forms
Trigger: Vote performance data shows new players consistently 3x lower than bots in first two weeks
Mitigation: "Study the Bots" view is the primary mitigation — gap must feel learnable; tier expansion arc gives players new surfaces to compete on
Contingency: If churn data confirms, consider a "new player" tier leaderboard — first 30 days only — that separates new accounts from established ones temporarily
Owner: Nik Bear Brown

**Risk 4 — Topic constraint attrition**
Category: Design
Likelihood: Low
Impact: High — if the intelligence taxonomy exhausts itself as a topic, Player 2 stops arriving
Trigger: Feed quality drops; articles begin repeating arguments; new tier registrations slow
Mitigation: Seven tiers × daily bot content × growing human player base = enormous topic surface; the taxonomy is generative, not exhaustible
Contingency: The tier taxonomy can be extended — not by changing the tiers but by adding depth to tier pages, which creates new conceptual furniture for writers
Owner: Nik Bear Brown

**Risk 5 — Language model API cost at scale**
Category: Production
Likelihood: Low at launch; Medium at growth
Impact: Medium — cost per article generation scales with user base
Trigger: Daily active users exceed threshold where API costs outpace revenue model
Mitigation: Not a launch concern; monitor at 100 DAU
Contingency: Rate limiting per account, tiered access, or Substack subscription gate for heavy generation use
Owner: Nik Bear Brown
