# AI in the Classroom: A Practical Workshop Series for Business School Faculty
### D'Amore-McKim School of Business · Northeastern University

A faculty member submits a course proposal with three AI-generated citations. Two of the studies do not exist. The journals are real. The authors are real. The papers are not. She had no framework for why it happened and no way to have caught it.

This is not an unusual case. It is the default failure mode of a confident tool used without a mental model — and it is happening in business schools everywhere, in course proposals, lecture updates, and assessment design, by faculty who were handed an integration mandate without being handed the training to execute it safely.

This workshop closes that gap in four sessions.

---

Business school faculty are being asked to integrate AI into their teaching, their assessments, and their own workflows — without training, without clear policy guidance, and without time to figure it out alone. The ask is real. The support is not.

The problem is not willingness. Faculty who have tried AI tools have encountered the full range of responses: output that is smooth and wrong, prompts that produce generic results no real course can use, assessments that feel increasingly porous, and policy language that lands somewhere between prohibition and surrender — neither of which prepares students for a professional world where these tools are already standard.

What faculty need is not awareness of AI. It is practical capability with it: a mental model for what the tool actually is, a method for directing it toward usable output, a protocol for knowing when to trust what it produces, and an approach to assessment and policy that holds up when students are already using it whether or not you have addressed it.

That is what this workshop builds. Four sessions. Four artifacts. One AI Teaching Portfolio built from your own courses in the room, ready to deploy.

## How the sessions work

Every session follows the same three-part structure: open with a case, demonstrate live, build something real before leaving.

Each session opens with a real failure or near-miss — not a definition, not a framework. Participants encounter the problem before they encounter the concept. The concept lands because they already feel why it matters. Core content is demonstrated in real AI tools in real time, including when the tools fail. The workshop portion of every session produces an artifact specific to each participant's own course. No hypotheticals. No homework. The artifact is built in the room.

## Session by session

**Session 1 — What You're Actually Working With**

*The question this session answers:* Why does AI give me wrong answers so confidently — and what do I do about that?

The opening case is the faculty member with the fabricated citations — the default failure mode that follows from using a confident tool without a mental model. Session 1 gives that mental model: the structural difference between pattern completion and knowledge retrieval, and why confidence of expression is uncorrelated with accuracy of content. The reliability question is made practical: which tasks are safe to delegate, which require verification before use, and which should never leave your hands. A live demonstration shows the same question asked three ways — and a live hallucination in a business domain, caught in real time.

The workshop produces the **Personal AI Reliability Map**: one page, specific to your discipline and your courses, built in the session.

---

**Session 2 — How to Direct It Well**

*The question this session answers:* Why do I keep getting mediocre output even when I try, and how do I fix that?

A Strategy faculty member asks AI to generate a case discussion for her MBA class. The output is smooth, generic, could apply to any industry in any decade. She concludes AI isn't useful for her teaching. The problem wasn't the tool. It was the instruction — which did exactly what it was asked to do, which was not what she needed. Session 2 teaches the five components of an instruction that produces usable output: the task, the audience, the inclusions, the exclusions, and the success standard. Live demonstration shows one weak prompt rebuilt through all five components, with output comparison. Business school applications covered: case discussion questions, assignment rubrics, exam questions at calibrated difficulty levels, research summaries for course prep.

The workshop produces the **Working Prompt Specification**: a tested prompt for one real course task, written, run, and revised in the session.

---

**Session 3 — How to Evaluate What It Gives You**

*The question this session answers:* How do I know when to trust AI output — and how do I push back on it when I shouldn't?

An Organizational Behavior faculty member uses AI to summarize current research on psychological safety for a lecture update. The summary is well-organized, smooth, and cites three papers that have been substantially challenged since publication. A student raises it in class. The faculty member had no verification practice — no framework for knowing which layer of the output to check. Session 3 introduces four layers every AI output contains, ordered by where each is most likely to fail in business school disciplines: facts, reasoning, framing, and omissions. The adversarial prompt — using AI to interrogate what AI just told you — is demonstrated live.

The workshop produces the **Discipline-Specific Verification Checklist**: what to check, how to check it, and what "good enough to use" looks like for your specific context.

---

**Session 4 — AI in Your Classroom: Assessment and Policy**

*The question this session answers:* What do I actually say to my students about AI, and how do I redesign my assessments so they hold up?

A Finance faculty member bans AI in her course. Her students use it anyway, undisclosed. Her assessments are unchanged. She is grading AI-generated work without knowing it, and her students are not developing the skills she believes she is developing. The policy failed not because students are dishonest — but because it was designed for a world that no longer exists. A prohibition without redesign is not a policy. It is a wish. Session 4 addresses the right question: not "did they use AI?" but "what did they have to do that AI cannot do for them?" Assessment redesign principles for a business school context — shifting from product to process, requiring specificity AI cannot fake, making AI use explicit and disclosed. Syllabus policy language that works: not prohibition, not open permission, but specification.

A volunteer participant's existing assignment is redesigned live in the room before and after.

The workshop produces the **Redesigned Assessment plus AI Policy Statement**: a revised real assignment and a draft policy paragraph ready for your syllabus.
